PROJECT / BACKGROUND

Why build a
caster at all?

The machine began as an answer to a very concrete research problem: making good solid-electrolyte films for sodium-ion batteries, cheaply and repeatably.

01 — The origin

It started in a battery lab.

The project grew directly out of work on NASICON (Na₃Zr₂Si₂PO₁₂) — a ceramic that conducts sodium ions and is one of the most promising solid electrolytes for next-generation, all-solid-state sodium-ion batteries. Sodium chemistry is attractive because it sidesteps the cost and supply pressure of lithium, using abundant, inexpensive raw materials.

To test a solid electrolyte electrochemically, you first need a membrane of it: a thin, uniform, defect-free sheet. In the lab, those sheets were being made by hand — and the results were inconsistent. Thickness varied across a single film, particles settled unevenly, and drying introduced cracks. The variable that mattered most, film uniformity, was the one hardest to control by hand.

02 — The stakes

Why solid electrolytes, why sodium.

A

Safer by design

Replacing the flammable liquid electrolyte of conventional cells with a solid ceramic removes a major fire-and-leak risk and enables denser cell architectures.

B

Abundant chemistry

Sodium is cheap and widely available. A working sodium solid-state cell is a strong candidate for grid-scale storage where lithium’s cost is prohibitive.

C

Thin is everything

Ionic resistance scales with membrane thickness. Getting the solid electrolyte reliably below 100 µm is what makes the cell performant — and that demands casting precision.

03 — The gap

Manual casting vs. a €30k commercial rig.

Once the requirement was clear — repeatable sub-100 µm films — the options were stark. Manual drawing couldn’t deliver the consistency. Commercial automated tape casters could, but their cost and closed, proprietary architecture put them well outside an undergraduate project budget and left no room to experiment with the mechanism itself.

So the decision was to build one. Not a scaled-down toy, but a genuinely capable, open-architecture instrument dimensioned around the real physics of casting NASICON slurry: constant shear rate, controlled bottom-up heat, and micron-scale gap adjustment.

The governing equation

Everything traces back to one relationship — the shear rate the slurry experiences, γ̇ = v / h, where v is the blade’s traverse velocity and h is the gap height. Ceramic slurries are shear-thinning and non-Newtonian, so any wobble in v changes the apparent viscosity mid-cast and scatters the film thickness. Holding v perfectly constant, and setting h precisely, is the whole job. The machine is the hardware answer to that equation.
