Fourteen design criteria, distilled into hard specifications, realised across four subsystems — each one tied back to the physics of casting a uniform film.

01 — Design criteria & specifications

From requirements to numbers.

Fourteen criteria were established and ranked with a binary dominance matrix. Safety ranked highest, followed by the three physics parameters that decide film quality: velocity stability, precision gap control, and thermal uniformity. Weight ranked last — a heavier machine damps vibration better, so mass was never minimised.

Micrometer heads adjusting the casting gap in 10 µm increments over a 0–5000 µm range.
Thermal range
A 200×200 mm mica bed reaching a steady-state ≥120 °C with uniform planar distribution.
Structural mass
High-density, vibration-absorbing base (MDF + heavy aluminium extrusion) to keep micro-stepping vibration below any amplitude that would disturb the wet film.
S-01 — Linear drive

Constant velocity, zero stutter.

Because slurry viscosity depends on shear rate γ̇ = v/h, the single most important mechanical requirement is a perfectly steady traverse velocity. A high-torque NEMA 23 stepper drives the carriage through a GT2 timing belt into dual T8 lead screws, riding on twin MGN12 linear rails for a near-zero-deflection path.

The TB6600 driver runs at 1600 micro-pulses per revolution — fine enough to erase the vibration that a coarse step would inject into the wet film. A velocity-locking scheme in firmware freezes the chosen speed at the moment casting starts, so nothing drifts mid-stroke.

S-02 — Doctor blade

The knob that sets thickness.

The doctor blade is where the gap height h is physically set. A 150 mm-wide blade carries a chamfered metering land, and two independent micrometer heads raise or lower it in 10 µm steps — directly dictating the shear-driven flow volume and therefore the wet-film thickness.

A reduced-order hydrodynamic model — power-law rheology with Arrhenius temperature dependence — established the usable gap window as 0.20–0.55 mm, producing 100–300 µm wet films that sinter down to the sub-100 µm membranes the cell design needs. The 10 µm adjustment resolution is deliberately matched to this window: fine enough to be the only meaningful source of thickness uncertainty.

S-03 — Heated vacuum chuck

Hold it flat. Heat it evenly.

The most demanding component in the machine — and the one that most directly protects film quality during drying.

During bottom-up heating, a flexible carrier film expands and wants to buckle off the hot surface. Wherever it lifts, thermal contact changes, the local solvent evaporation rate changes, and the film develops thickness gradients that look exactly like a blade error on inspection. The fix is active vacuum hold-down.

The chuck is a bimetallic aluminium assembly: a 20 mm Al 5083 base carrying internal vacuum channels and an O-ring seal groove, capped by a 5 mm Al 2045 top plate perforated with 117 micro-holes. Negative pressure draws the carrier flat and keeps it in intimate thermal contact through the entire cast-and-dry cycle.

Underneath, an 800 W mica heater driven by a PID controller holds the bed setpoint. A finite-element check at 100 °C showed the coating surface staying within a 1.6 °C gradient across the working area, with the heavy 20 mm base acting as a thermal spreader. The two aluminium alloys were chosen for closely matched expansion coefficients, so the plates don’t shear apart as they heat.

S-04 — Forced-convection drying

The fans aren’t optional.

A pair of fans in the pivoting lid do far more than cool electronics — they are a process requirement. A convective mass-transfer analysis (flat-plate, laminar, using the Sherwood-number correlation) modelled how solvent leaves the drying film.

The result was decisive. In a sealed chamber, ethanol vapour saturates the air and drying stalls near 24% residual solvent no matter how long you wait. Switch the fans on and the mass-transfer coefficient jumps by roughly an order of magnitude, drying the film to near-completion in about 400 seconds. The same analysis flagged a safety limit — more on that in the electronics.
