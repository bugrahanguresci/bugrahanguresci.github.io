01 — The machine

A laboratory instrument, not a lab hack.

Tape casting is how ceramic and battery films are made: a blade meters a liquid slurry into a thin, uniform layer that dries into a flexible “green tape.” Done by hand, the pulling speed wobbles, the film thickness scatters, and defects appear. Commercial automated casters solve this — at a price that puts them out of reach for most research labs.

This project closes that gap. It is an open-architecture, fully automated caster that holds a constant casting speed, adjusts the blade gap in 10 µm steps, and heats the substrate from below through a custom vacuum chuck — all built for a fraction of commercial cost using accessible components and university workshop machining.

02 — Four engineered subsystems

Every subsystem solves a specific failure mode.

The design didn’t start from a parts list — it started from the physics of what goes wrong when you cast a film, and worked backward to the hardware that prevents it.

S-01

Linear drive

NEMA 23 stepper on twin MGN12 rails and dual T8 screws holds a stutter-free, constant traverse — killing the velocity swings that ruin manual casts.

Kinematics → S-02

Doctor blade

150 mm blade with a chamfered metering land and twin micrometer heads for ±10 µm gap control — the knob that sets film thickness.

Blade physics → S-03

Heated vacuum chuck

Bimetallic aluminium plate with 117 EDM-drilled micro-holes clamps the carrier flat while heating it evenly from below.

Thermal design → S-04

Control & safety

Arduino finite-state machine, PID heat control, and a fully isolated 220 V protective cascade with EMI-filtered entry.

FIG. 03 — Physical casting trial, wet film on carrier substrate

From model to material.

Direct timing experiments confirmed no measurable deviation between the predicted and observed carriage speeds. The four-state firmware produced deterministic, repeatable cycles and eliminated the motor stutter that plagued earlier prototype versions.

A velocity-sensitivity analysis showed that a 5% speed fluctuation introduces thickness errors above ±5 µm at low traverse rates — quantitatively justifying the microstepping drive as the mechanism that suppresses the dominant source of thickness scatter.
